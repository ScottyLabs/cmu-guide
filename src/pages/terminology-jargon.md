Note there are two different kinds of abbreviations that vary only in how they are verbally spoken (not how they are written). Here is how terms appear in this document:

Initialism: pronounced letter by letter
- Example: **FBI, initialism** (pronounce as: Eff-Bee-Aye)

Acronym: pronounced as a single word
- Example: **NATO, acronym** (pronounce as: Nay-Toe)

If something is not an abbreviation (i.e. labelled as either), then it's just pronounced as a normal word
- Example: **SoHo** (Sow-Hoe)

## Locations
### Region
- **Pitt:** the University of Pittsburgh (NOT the city! Locals will yell at you...)
- **PIT:** Pittsburgh International Airport, only seen in text due to phonetic indistinguishability with Pitt.
- **PGH:** the City of Pittsburgh, initialism
- **PA:** Pennsylvania, initialism
- **Squill/Squirrel Hill:** Neighborhood of Pittsburgh, quick 61A/B/C/D bus ride from campus going east on Forbes Ave, shopping/dining destination
  - CMU main campus is in Squirrel Hill (North)
- **Oakland:** Neighborhood of Pittsburgh, quick 61A/B/C/D bus ride from campus going west on Forbes Ave, shopping/dining destination, location of Pitt
  - Especially to CMU students, Oakland is definitively *not* CMU and refers to the area around Pitt. CMU is popularly misconceived by visitors as being in Oakland
- **Wushi:**: Wushiland, a notoriously popular boba shop (the closest one to campus!)

### CMU campus
- **(C)UC:** University Center, initialism (acronym if making an immature joke)
- **Rev:** Revolution Noodle, the hot pot restaurant in the UC
- **ABP:** Au Bon Pain, Panera-like bakery-cafe fast casual restaurant in the UC, initialism
- **Stack'd:** Pittsburgh-local chain of burger joints. Full restaurant in Oakland, but the term almost always refers to the CMU on-campus restaurant Stack'd Underground in Morewood Gardens' basement which serves a subset of its parent's dishes, along with cheesy furnishings from a related chain "Melt'd." The aforementioned Morewood location used to be known as just "The Underground" or "(the) UG" (initialism) prior to 2023 when it was an independently-owned outfit; the decor/signage is unchanged from this era. Extremely rarely referred to as either term.
- **The Cut:** North-south lawn, between the UC and Purnell (buildings have matching facades)
- **The Mall:** East-west lawn, between Wean + Doherty and Porter + Baker. Hamerschlag Hall (ECE) with the iconic chimney in all the CMU photos anchors its westernmost point
- **The Fence:** the paint-able landmark at the middle of campus where The Cut meets The Mall. (In?)famous for its surrounding culture of camping, negotiations, and intense guarding, driven by strict requirements for legal painting such as only beginning to paint between midnight and sunrise, that each attempt must lay fresh paint on the *whole* fence, and only use hand-painting with simple paint brushes; otherwise, an attempt is considered vandalism. At least two students in the surrounding gravel area are required to guard the fence, and thus initiate an attempt to paint it.

## Academics
- **AIV:** Academic Integrity Violation, many classes (122, 213) are notorious for high numbers of AIV accusations. Initialism. An AIV is only given once a formal accusation has been made by a professor to you, and you declined to contest it or failed to do so in a review board assembled by **OCSI** (Office of Community Standards and Integrity). A sliding scale of consequences arises from severity (including having multiple AIVs), but the first case almost always only carries the university-wide consequence of being put on your record (not shared externally though), and that another will be more serious. Any AIV always involves an in-class grade penalty, which vary from a 0, negative points on the assignment, all the way to losing a letter grade or failing the course.  
- **GenEd:** Class fulfilling General Education requirements (vary per major)
- **Mini:** Half-semester course
- **Unit(s):** CMU credit unit, equivalent to 3 typical US college credit hours, meant to generally indicate how many hours/week of work a class requires
- **FCE(s):** Faculty Course Evaluation(s), initialism--**you the student(s)** complete evaluations of your courses at the end of every semester, which are reviewed by faculty, available to all CMU students for past semesters, and which include an estimate of your average workload per week. **Term often used after a number** to indicate the **average of all students** i.e. "This class is 15 FCE" = 15 hour/week workload on average for a student, averaged across all students' responses
- **Prereq(uisite):** Course(s) you must have satisfactorily completed prior to being allowed to register for a given course
- **Coreq(uisite):** Course(s) in which you must be enrolled simultaneously with a given course. Strictly looser condition than prereqs; if you have already completed the corequisite course, you automatically fulfill the coreq. 

## People/Departments
- **Andrew ID:** Your CMU login and main identifier
- **RA:** Resident Assistant, the upperclassmen responsible for you in your dorm floor/section
- **CA:** Community Advisor, the upperclassmen responsible for the student leadership element of the entire dorm, who manages RAs and to whom RAs report
- **Housefellow:** Professional, CMU-employed adults responsible for an entire dorm, who are notably your Student Affairs contact point if you're living in on-campus housing. They are usually first to contact you if they think or hear that you're having struggles of any sort. 
- **(Division of) Student Affairs:** WIP describing what they do and why they'd get involved with you as a whole is difficult because they have such a diversity of departments under their scope
- **The HUB:** CMU financial department, including financial aid
- **SIT:** Very frequently referred to by their old name SLICE, this is the office of Student Involvement and Traditions. You'll interface with them if you're in any organization's leadership or significantly involved member.

## Infrastructure and popular culture
- **Canvas:** `canvas.cmu.edu`, main hub for most courses' content
- **`Andrew:`** the computing and network systems around campus. Originally was a pioneering networked system in the 1980s when CS was new; nowadays term is commodified and just refers to the modern commodity systems around campus, and some legacy descendant components of the original architecture
- **AFS:** Andrew File System, a unique campus-wide networked file system on which you are allocated 5GB of storage by default, only accessible on CMU Linux machines
- **Autolab:** CMU's in-house automated assignment grading system, mostly for programming assignments
- **Gradescope:** popular all-purpose assignment submission platform usually used by classes if not using Canvas submissions

### General CMU culture
- **(Spring) Carnival:** largest CMU annual tradition; a carnival (American fair rides, food, festivities) is set-up on the Mall, the Cut, and surrounding areas.
  - **Buggy:** Occasionally called by its formal name, Sweepstakes, this is the CMU Carnival tradition of Greek and independent organizations racing "buggies" which are small, low, aerodynamic vehicles, competitively built and practiced with generational and new knowledge throughout the course of an entire year. Famously involves orgs desperately recruiting freshmen who are short in height (approx. 5 feet or less) to be their drivers.
  - **Booth:** Multi-story human-sized structures built by organizations around themes of their choosing. **Doghouses** are a much smaller, easier variant. **Midway** is the formal construction area set-up on the CFA parking lot, closed off during construction, and where safety requirements for builders are strictly followed.
  - **S'n'S Carnival slot:** the Carnival play, always a musical, run by **Scotch and Soda**, initialism S'n'S, a theatre org for **non-majors** (i.e. not professional School of Drama). Famous for unbelievable grinds in its performers, and in constructing and deconstructing the stage and audience seating area in the UC Rangos ballroom. Their largest show by effort and audience numbers every year. 

### Famous classes
Familiarity with these will greatly reduce your confusion and increase your sociability. As you'd expect, most of these are tech related. Note that students often drop the department code when talking about these since they're so notorious (i.e. instead of 15-122, they just say 122). Students almost never refer to iconic classes by name, but rather by number; any exceptions will be listed by the name most often used. All of these classes have sub-cultures around them because of their size, instructor devotion, legacy, and/or TA passion
- **Concepts (of Math(ematics)):** Essentially an introduction to discrete math and proof-writing. Infamous for being difficult and unapproachable to students without competitive math backgrounds and thus, arguably, educational or perhaps socioeconomic privilege. Two different versions: 21-127 for everyone outside of CS and Math, 15-151/21-128 (cross-listed, so the same course) for CS and Math respectively. CS students will also refer to the course by the number, 151.
- **15-112:** Mainstream (but non-trivial!) introduction course to CS, using Python and an online platform called CS Academy. Completion is equivalent to a 5 on AP CS A
- **15-122:** Rigorous foundational course in imperative, provably correct/safe programming and data structures, accessible after 15-112. Famous for its in-house `C0` language, a language closely related to C, to which the course transitions to in the end after using `C0` for memory safety/correctness instruction. Notorious for harsh and widespread AIV accusations, no-nonsense grading policies, and for the lack of flexibility or grace from instructors, though recently this has been improving
- **NoL (initialism)/Nature of Language:** Now renamed to "Introduction to Linguistics" but almost always called by its famous old name. Iconic GenEd, especially when taught by Prof. Christina Bjorndahl who is iconic for her incredible theatrics, sociability, and kindness. Popular with students across disciplines, but especially tech. Introductory course to the Linguistics major/minor. Course number 80-180 but never called this by students. Arguably also infamous for tricking CIT students into thinking it's an easy GenEd, when it's not impossible but not at all a free class.
- **15/18-213 (513/613 for grad students):** Introduction to Computer Systems. Taught in 100% C. (In?)famous for its labs like `malloc`, `proxy`, and `cache`, which collectively contribute half of students' grades. First mostly project-based CS course students encounter. ECE and CS have their own versions, claimed to be for slightly tailoring content, however the real reason is artificial/department politics in that ECE wanted to stop bleeding money to CS for another core course common to both of them (CMU allocates some tuition money to schools/departments based off of course enrollment). No difference whatsoever between them in any subsequent course's prereqs; they are completely interchangeable. Very rarely (old documentation?) known as the initialism *ICS* for its official course title.
  - **CS:APP** (Computer Systems: A Programmer's Perspective) is its famous textbook written by our own Profs. Randy Bryant and David O'Hallaron, the de-facto gold standard used by practically every computer systems course in the world. I've heard it verbalized once as an initialism, and the other time with "CS" letter by letter and "APP" as a single word
- **Interp:** Sometimes used as an acronym for the full-year first-year writing (FYW) course option

## Student life
- **(meal) block:** Prepaid dining plan meals. A block varies in value from Breakfast, Lunch, Dinner, and Late Nite, where the value is strictly Breakfast\< Lunch/Late Nite\< Dinner, even if the cash values increase slightly every year accounting for inflation. Can be sold on the **Block Market** Discord server for (usually electronic) payment at market rates, always below official value since upperclassmen would then just outright buy meals. Often part of freshmen-upperclassmen culture, since freshmen often give meal blocks to upperclass friends as tokens of friendship.
  - Cash equivalent value followup: all on-campus restaurants can technically make a sale of any combination of items less than or equal to the meal block equivalent price, whether it's an official block meal combo or not. Often used at Millie's to get vast amounts of ice cream. However, it's entirely at the discretion of the cashier whether they will let you make a cash equivalent order with a meal block; I'm not sure if it's technically disallowed or not. 
- **Grubhub/GH:** platform exclusively used to place food orders from mobile devices to on-campus restaurants. If on a dining plan, it can be linked here to spend meal blocks.
